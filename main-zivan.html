Agriculture accounts for 70% of global freshwater usage, with corn being one of the most water intensive crops. Seeing the environmental and economic challenges brought up by current irrigation systems, our team embarked on a mission to design a sustainable and innovative irrigation system for Canadian corn farming. As the Scientific Design Lead, I played a significant role in developing and testing solutions that minimize water usage while maintaining crop yield and quality. The project aimed to address water conservation, reduce environmental impact, and ensure economic feasibility, all within a $100 budget constraint. This project was especially important given Canadaâ€™s increasing focus on sustainable farming methods.

To achieve these objectives, our team followed a pattern of brainstorming ideas, creating prototypes, and testing them. We began with brainstorming and stop and engage ideation techniques, generating over 20 potential solutions. Using a weighted evaluation matrix, we assessed these ideas based on metrics such as cost, water efficiency, and durability, ultimately selecting two promising designs: the Water Train and Concrete Half-Pipe Systems. The Water Train is an autonomous and modular system that delivers water directly to the base of the plant, reducing waste and labor requirements. The Concrete Half-Pipe, on the other hand, channels water efficiently while withstanding heavy farm equipment. Both designs were thoroughly tested using simulations and physical prototypes to ensure they would work well in real world conditions. After testing and prototyping, ultimately the concrete half pipe was chosen as the final solution due to its low maintenance cost, water saving and durability.

This project opened an opportunity for both engineering and personal growth. As the lead for the scientific design, I created testing procedures, evaluated the riskiest assumptions and aided in other aspects of the project such as the ideation and prototyping of the systems helping me gain hands-on experience with prototyping tools and simulation software, enhancing my technical skills. Working closely with my teammates, I learned to balance the technological side of the project with user focused design, making sure the solutions were practical for Canadian farmers. Beyond the technical aspects, I gained a deeper understanding of the environmental impact of agricultural practices, which emphasized the critical role of sustainability in engineering.

The impact of this project goes beyond the technical solutions alone. By combining engineering with broader perspectives, we created a project that promotes freshwater conservation, supports local ecosystems, and strengthens food security. Looking back on this experience, I feel great achievement and personal growth as this project made me realise my passion for this course and solving engineering problems with a team.
